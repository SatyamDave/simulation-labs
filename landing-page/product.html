What we ship

A swarm of behavioral agents attempts your real flow — sign up, check out, cancel — and records, for each one, exactly where it abandons. You give us a URL and a task; we run a set of degraded computer-use agents against it in parallel and hand back the evidence of where the flow broke down and for whom.

How a run works

Each agent runs the same loop, driven by a pre-trained computer-use model that reads a screenshot and emits an action. We take a screenshot of the live page, perturb that frame to model the agent's constraints, let the model decide on an action, apply any motor noise to the coordinates, and execute the click or keystroke verbatim on the page. Every step emits a thumbnail and a short caption, so a run reads as a trace rather than a black box. The loop repeats until the task succeeds, or the step budget runs out, or the time budget runs out, or a stuck-detector calls it. There is no live widget to babysit — you start a run and read the result.

The perturbations are mechanical and dimension-preserving, so degrading what the model sees never moves the coordinates it clicks: Gaussian blur and downscaling for reduced acuity, a colour-vision filter for lost colour cues, Gaussian jitter on the click coordinates, hard step and wall-clock limits, and a small mobile viewport.

The behavioral models

Eight agents, described by how they behave rather than by any condition:

  • Power user — a fluent baseline with no perturbation, the control for how the flow behaves when nothing is in the way.
  • AI agent — also unperturbed, a control for whether your site is usable by an agent at all.
  • Can't read small or low-contrast text — the frame is blurred and downscaled before the model sees it.
  • Clicks land off-target — coordinate jitter at σ=14px on every click.
  • Colour-only cues vanish — a deutan colour-vision filter at high severity.
  • In a hurry on a small phone — a 390×844 viewport, mild jitter, and a 30-second deadline.
  • Reads labels literally, mistrusts icons — mild blur, small jitter, and a prompt that constrains it to literal reading.
  • Idioms and jargon slow them down — a literal-reading prompt under time pressure.

What you get

Every run produces a stepped survival curve (how many agents were still trying at each step), an abandonment heatmap of where clicks and give-ups clustered on the page, video receipts of each session, and cloned-voice exit interviews grounded in what each agent actually did. We also compute a 0–100 Simulation Score — a composite where a completed task scores full marks and a failure earns partial credit for how far it got — plus per-agent stats including a rage-click metric, and analytics that let you compare the score across releases.

These are rank-order signals, not forecasts: they tell you which page is worse and which fix helped, not what your conversion rate will be. In one before/after measured in rehearsal on a bundled test form, a fixed version scored 69→100 against the original — an illustration of the delta a run can surface, not a promise of any particular number. The accessibility view is labelled as evidence of risk, not an automated conformance verdict.

Who it's for

Growth and CRO teams looking for where a funnel leaks; product teams pressure-testing a launch before it ships; and QA or CI pipelines that want a behavioral check to run against a build the way a test suite does.
