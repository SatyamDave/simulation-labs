The method

Overview

Simulation Labs drives a swarm of pretrained computer-use agents around a live browser and measures where behavioral friction causes task abandonment. Each agent is an instance of a screenshot-in / action-out vision-language model (VLM) whose perception and actuation channels are transformed by deterministic signal-processing operators before pixels reach the model and after its coordinates leave it. We do not train a model and we do not prompt an agent to behave differently: we intervene only in the I/O channels. The observable consequence, task completion or abandonment at a specific pixel under a specific behavioral condition, is aggregated into a survival curve, an abandonment heatmap, per-persona statistics, and a 0–100 friction score. Every output is a rank-order risk signal and evidence of friction, never a conformance verdict or a conversion forecast.

The thesis is empirical: prompted LLM personas match the real people they stand in for only about 11.86% of the time (Park et al., 2025, arXiv:2503.20749). A stated preference is not a click. So rather than ask a model to opine, we degrade the channel and watch behavior. We claim mechanical fidelity, that the perceptual and motor limits we impose are real, measured, and reproducible, not psychological fidelity. Working end-to-end; frozen-contract five-module architecture; 400+ tests; MIT-licensed; built at the H Company Computer Use Hackathon.

1 · Computer-use agents: screenshot-to-action grounding

A computer-use agent is a policy π(a | o, g, h) mapping an on-screen observation o, a goal g, and interaction history h to an action in a discrete-plus-continuous action space. In the pixel-only regime we target, the observation is the rendered screenshot alone, with no DOM and no structured element tree, so the policy must solve visual grounding: converting a referring expression ("the Sign up button") into executable screen coordinates. This is the capability gap that dominates end-to-end failure. On OSWorld, humans complete >72% of tasks while the strongest agents at publication reached ~12%, a gap the authors attribute primarily to GUI grounding and operational knowledge (Xie et al., 2024); WebArena reports similar headroom over 812 execution-verified web tasks (Zhou et al., 2023).

Two grounding paradigms are in use. Intrinsic grounding emits coordinates directly: SeeClick (Cheng et al., 2024) fine-tunes a Qwen-VL backbone and introduces the ScreenSpot cross-platform benchmark, demonstrating screenshot-only localization without structured metadata; CogAgent (Hong et al., 2023) pairs a low-resolution global encoder with a high-resolution 1120×1120 cross-attention branch so small glyphs remain resolvable. Extrinsic grounding (Set-of-Mark; Yang et al., 2023) overlays numbered marks from a segmentation model, reducing localization to discrete label selection; SeeAct (Zheng et al., 2024) isolates the bottleneck directly, GPT-4V succeeds on 51.1% of live tasks when actions are manually grounded, implicating localization rather than planning. We use intrinsic grounding so the perceptual operators of §2 act on exactly the signal the model consumes; a mark overlay would leak an un-degraded structural channel and defeat the experiment.

We consume a pretrained Action-VLM (H Company's Holo family; Surfer-H, 2025) as the policy and supply the browser, the degradation operators, and the measurement harness. The parsed action space is a fixed enum, click(x,y) / write / scroll / go_back / refresh / wait / goto / restart / answer, executed in the standard perceive–decide–act loop of §Run loop.

2 · Perceptual degradation as signal processing

We transform the perception channel with deterministic, dimension-preserving operators applied to the screenshot before it is encoded. Each is a characterized image-processing filter; none introduces stochasticity in the pixel path, so a fixed (persona, page) pair produces byte- identical model input on replay.

Gaussian blur is an isotropic low-pass filter. Convolution with a 2-D Gaussian kernel has a Gaussian Fourier transform:

g(x,y) = (1 / 2πσ²) exp(−(x²+y²) / 2σ²) ⇌ G(u,v) = exp(−2π²σ²(u²+v²))

Because G decreases monotonically in radial spatial frequency, high- frequency content (fine text edges, 1-px borders, low-contrast affordances) is attenuated in proportion to σ while low-frequency layout survives. The blur σ is therefore a direct knob on the spatial-frequency bandwidth of perception. Its behavioral relevance rests on the classical result that visual detection is mediated by independent, spatial-frequency-selective channels and governed by the contrast sensitivity function (Campbell & Robson, 1968): content whose distinguishing signal lives above the cutoff is effectively removed from the model's input.

Downsampling and the Nyquist limit. The resolution operator downsamples by a factor s and restores the original dimensions in place. Sampling at rate fs faithfully represents only content below the Nyquist frequency fs/2 (Shannon, 1949); reducing the rate by s lowers the Nyquist limit by s, so features finer than ~s pixels are lost and un-prefiltered content aliases into spurious low-frequency artifacts. Colour is applied as a fixed 3×3 linear operator M(ρ) on linearized RGB, parameterized by severity ρ ∈ [0,1] (channel-transformation matrices, after Machado et al., 2009), remapping how information carried by specific colour-channel contrasts projects onto the encoded image.

Dimension preservation is the golden rule. Blur changes pixel values only; downsample resizes down then back up in place; the colour operator is per-pixel. All four leave the buffer size (W, H) invariant, which keeps the coordinate-denormalization target (§Coordinate space) equal to the true viewport and makes runs byte- deterministic in the perception path. The pipeline asserts img.size == original_size before returning.

3 · Actuation noise and targeting

The actuation channel adds stochastic offset to the model's chosen coordinate before the click is issued, modeling imprecise pointing. The framework is Fitts's law and its endpoint-scatter extensions. Fitts (1954) showed movement time is linear in an index of difficulty, a log ratio of target distance A to width W; in MacKenzie's (1992) Shannon form:

ID = log₂(A / W + 1)  [bits]  MT = a + b·ID

The stochastic optimized-submovement model (Meyer et al., 1988) explains the logarithmic speed–accuracy trade-off as a ballistic submovement plus corrective submovements whose variability scales with velocity, the mechanistic origin of the scatter we inject. We model the executed endpoint as isotropic bivariate normal about the intended coordinate, giving a closed-form hit probability for a square target of extent W (Wobbrock et al., 2008):

(X, Y) ~ 𝒩((x₀, y₀), σ²I₂)  Phit(W) = [ 2Φ(W / 2σ) − 1 ]²

Monte-Carlo integration at σ ≈ 14 px reproduces the sharp size-dependence of the miss rate. The closed form evaluates to 21.8% at W = 44 and 5.3% at W = 62, matching simulation; the 24-px point is within Monte-Carlo tolerance.

For a persona with 14-px endpoint scatter, targets below ~44 px carry steep mis-hit risk that becomes negligible only past ~62 px, a rank-order signal on control sizing arrived at purely from the pointing statistics, not a conversion prediction (offline Monte-Carlo, 20,000 trials/cell; illustrative).

4 · Abandonment as survival analysis

An agent either completes the task or terminates at some step. We treat "steps until abandonment" as a time-to-event variable, which handles the right-censoring our budgets impose: a run that hits the step or time budget without abandoning is censored (its abandonment step, if any, exceeds the budget). Infrastructure errors are excluded from the risk set entirely; only genuine behavioral terminations count as events. Censoring is what distinguishes this from a naive completion rate, which silently misattributes budget-truncated runs.

With ordered event steps ti, di abandonments and ni personas still at risk at ti, the product-limit (Kaplan–Meier, 1958) estimator and Greenwood variance are:

Ŝ(t) = ∏ti ≤ t (1 − di / ni)  Var(Ŝ(t)) = Ŝ(t)² ∑ti ≤ t di / [ni(ni − di)]

Ŝ(t) is exactly the survival curve we report: the fraction of the swarm still progressing after t steps, with censored personas kept in the denominator until their censoring step. The complementary hazard h(t) is the instantaneous conditional abandonment rate, with S(t) = exp(−∫₀ᵗ h(u) du); steps where the empirical hazard spikes are the funnel locations where friction concentrates, and combined with the recorded termination pixel they drive the abandonment heatmap. To compare two designs or cohorts we fit a Cox proportional-hazards model (Cox, 1972), h(t|z) = h₀(t) exp(βᵀz), reporting hazard ratios strictly as rank-order comparative signals.

5 · ICP behavioral calibration

Personas are behavioral (Rushed, Distracted, Misclick-prone, First-timer, Mobile-thumb, Slow-connection, plus Fluent and AI-agent controls), each a vector θ of the §2–§3 channel parameters: blur σ, resolution factor s, colour severity ρ, viewport (W,H), endpoint noise σclick, and step / time budgets. Calibration is choosing the distribution over θ so the swarm's aggregate behavior matches a customer's ICP segments. This is configuration over a fixed pretrained policy, not model training: no weights are updated and no gradients flow into the agent.

We represent a segment as θ ~ p(θ | φ) with φ the calibration vector; a swarm run is a Monte-Carlo sample and a reported statistic is a Monte-Carlo estimate of the segment-level expectation:

𝔼θ~p(·|φ)[ m(θ) ] ≈ (1/K) ∑k=1ⁿ m(θk)

To control estimator variance at fixed swarm size K, parameters are drawn by Latin Hypercube Sampling (McKay, Beckman & Conover, 1979), which stratifies each marginal and yields lower-variance estimates than simple random sampling, the reason a modest swarm produces stable curves. Choosing φ is a Bayesian calibration of a computer model to observed data (Kennedy & O'Hagan, 2001): fit φ so simulated aggregates match segment targets, carrying calibration uncertainty forward into the reported intervals; validity is checked under the standard verification-and-validation framework (Sargent, 2013). Because the personas are calibrated configurations of a degraded-channel simulation, all downstream numbers are rank-order friction signals and evidence of risk under a stated behavioral condition, not conformance verdicts and not conversion forecasts.

Architecture

A five-module frozen-contract design. Every message crossing a module boundary is a Pydantic v2 model or a @runtime_checkable Protocol in shared/ghostpanel_contracts/contracts.py (frozen at CONTRACT_VERSION = "1.0.0"), never a bare dict. The modules are engine (perturbation transforms, Holo clients, persona agent and loader), runner (PlaywrightSessionRunner: the per-session loop, video, thumbnails, stuck/success detection, network policy), server (a FastAPI composition root that fans out over personas with asyncio.gather and pushes a WebSocket event stream), report (SurvivalReportBuilder: survival curve, heatmap, score), and voice (GradiumVoiceEngine: cloned-voice exit interviews with optional Claude narration). One Chromium instance is shared; each persona runs as its own asyncio task in an isolated browser context.

The run loop

One session drives a single persona from the target URL to a terminal outcome. Each step captures a screenshot, hands a perturbed copy to the model, jitters the returned coordinates, and executes the action verbatim, emitting a StepEvent every step. 

for step in range(persona.max_steps):
    png    = page.screenshot()                 # true viewport, un-perturbed
    frame  = perceive(png, persona)             # downscale -> blur -> colour (dims preserved)
    action = holo.navigate(frame, task, hist)   # policy returns an Action (0..1000 grid)
    action = actuate(action, persona, w, h)     # add endpoint noise, clamp to viewport
    emit StepEvent(caption, thumbnail, x, y)    # live grid tile

    if action is ANSWER and success_predicate:  outcome = SUCCESS;      break
    if is_stuck(history):                        outcome = STUCK;        break
    execute_action(page, action); settle()
    if success_predicate:                        outcome = SUCCESS;      break
    if sim_seconds >= persona.deadline_s:        outcome = TIME_BUDGET;  break
else:
    outcome = STEP_BUDGET

A session ends in exactly one outcome: success (a claimed completion verified against a predicate, receipts not vibes), step_budget, time_budget, stuck (a no-progress detector), or error (infra failure, excluded from statistics). Time is a simulated persona clock (page loads, waits, actions, a fixed per-step think cost) that excludes inference latency and rate-limiter queueing, so the time verdict is rate-limit-independent.

Coordinate space

Grounding VLMs in the Qwen-VL lineage represent coordinates on a resolution-independent [0, 1000) normalized grid, decoupling the coordinate head from input resolution. The client denormalizes to true viewport pixels against the image it sent and clamps to the viewport, so the runner executes verbatim with page.mouse.click(x, y):

xpx = (xnorm / 1000)·W  ypx = (ynorm / 1000)·H

# Verified live: a button whose true centre is (547, 430) in a 1280x800 shot
model returns  Click(426, 536)        # 0-1000 normalized grid
denormalize    426/1000 * 1280 = 545  # x, rounded
               536/1000 *  800 = 429  # y, rounded
runner clicks  (545, 429)             # true viewport pixels, back on target

This is why §2's operators must preserve pixel dimensions: the denormalization target must remain the real viewport. Inference runs at temperature 0; a shared token-bucket limiter paces the swarm to the RPM budget; the parser repairs known malformed shapes (key-dropped coordinates, short forms) so a run keeps progressing.

Outputs & scoring

A finished run produces the stepped survival curve (§4), the abandonment heatmap (termination pixels), and per-persona statistics: mean and p95 latency, blocked_actions, and max_repeated_action, the longest run of identical captions (a rage-click proxy). The composite Simulation Score is 0–100, equal weight per non-error persona: 

credit(persona) = 1.0                                   if outcome == SUCCESS
                = min(0.5, 0.5 * steps_survived / max_steps)   otherwise
score = round(100 * sum(credit) / count(non-error personas))

A success earns full credit; a failure earns partial credit for distance travelled, capped at 0.5, so dying at step 2 costs more than dying at step 25. A separate agent-readiness score is derived from the unimpaired AI-agent control: if an unimpaired agent cannot finish, the flow is not agent-usable. Every figure is reported with its behavioral condition and its calibration uncertainty; none is a conformance verdict.

Policy & safety

Runs are browse-only. A YAML request policy (a NemoClaw mirror) is enforced in-process at the Playwright network layer: every request is intercepted and anything the policy denies, including form POSTs, is aborted before it leaves the machine (fail-closed). We do not complete real purchases or submissions on third-party sites; a persona that claims completion after a blocked submission is not scored as a success. The full GPU sandbox (NVIDIA OpenShell / on-device Nemotron) is out of scope on a laptop, so the policy is the in-process network gate, not the full sandbox.
