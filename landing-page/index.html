01 — Vision

Synthetic users that do, not say

Synthetic-user research has quietly become a way to interview a model instead of a person. You describe a persona, ask what it thinks of your checkout, and it answers — fluently, agreeably, and at a comfortable distance from anything it would actually have done. The answers are shallow and people-pleasing, a pattern the Nielsen Norman Group has written about plainly. When prompted LLM personas were measured against the real people they were meant to stand in for, the match was around 11.86% (arXiv 2503.20749). A survey is not a user; a stated preference is not a click.

01 / method

Mechanical fidelity, not psychological fidelity

We are not reconstructing a human personality inside a language model. What we can reproduce faithfully are the constraints on the channel between a user and the screen: how sharply they see it, how precisely their clicks land, how little time and patience they bring. So we take a pre-trained computer-use model — a system that reads a screenshot and emits an action — and mechanically degrade its perception and action rather than prompt it to act impaired. We blur the frame, add Gaussian noise to the click coordinates, cap the number of steps, shrink the viewport, strip colour cues.

It is a deliberately narrow claim. We do not say these agents feel what a person feels or reason as they reason. We say the perceptual and motor limits we impose are real, measured, and reproducible even though the personality is synthetic — a smaller promise than the field usually makes, and one we can keep.

02 / goal

What we're building toward

We want the behavioral check to become a normal part of shipping — something you run before release the way you run a test suite, to see who fails your site before your users do. Not a forecast of conversion, and not a compliance verdict, but a rank-order signal: which page is worse, which fix helped, where abandonment clusters.

S. Dave

In an offline Monte-Carlo of our tremor actuation (20,000 trials/cell), a 14px jitter misses a 44px target 20.5% of the time and needs ≥62px targets to hold the miss-rate under 5%.

04 — Docs

How it works

The engine (internal package ghostpanel) drives H Company's Holo — a pre-trained, screenshot-in / action-out model (holo3-1-35b-a3b, which we consume, not train) — around a real browser via Playwright. Each step: take a screenshot, perceive (perturb the frame), get an action from the model, actuate (add tremor jitter), execute the click verbatim, emit an event. The loop ends on success, a step budget, a time budget, or the stuck-detector.

It's a five-module, frozen-contract design (engine · runner · server · report · voice) where modules talk only through Pydantic contracts. Coordinates from the model arrive on a 0–1000 grid and are denormalised to true viewport pixels. Every run produces a survival curve, an abandonment heatmap, per-persona stats, and a 0–100 Simulation Score. Findings are a rank-order signal and "evidence of risk", never a conformance verdict. Working end-to-end; 173 tests; MIT-licensed.
