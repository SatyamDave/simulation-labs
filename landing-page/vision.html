Synthetic users that do, not say

Synthetic-user research has quietly become a way to interview a model instead of a person. You describe a persona, you ask it what it thinks of your checkout flow, and it answers — fluently, agreeably, and at a comfortable distance from anything it would actually have done. The answers are shallow and people-pleasing, a pattern the Nielsen Norman Group has written about plainly. When researchers measured prompted LLM personas against the behavior of the real people they were meant to stand in for, the match was around 11.86% (arXiv 2503.20749). A survey is not a user. A stated preference is not a click.

Saying is cheap; doing is the evidence

People are unreliable narrators of their own friction. They will tell you a form was fine and then, in the recording, abandon it at the third field. Our bet is to stop asking and start watching: point an agent at your real flow with a real goal, and record where it either finishes or gives up. The persona doesn't opine about your checkout — it attempts it, and either completes the task or rage-quits at a specific pixel. Failure is the product.

Mechanical fidelity, not psychological fidelity

We are not trying to reconstruct a human personality inside a language model. We think that is both hard and, for this purpose, beside the point. What we can reproduce faithfully are constraints on the channel between a user and the screen: how sharply they see it, how precisely their clicks land, how little time and patience they bring. So we take a pre-trained computer-use model — a system that reads a screenshot and emits an action — and mechanically degrade its perception and action, rather than prompt it to act impaired. We blur the frame, add Gaussian noise to the click coordinates, cap the number of steps, shrink the viewport, strip colour cues.

This is a deliberately narrow claim. We do not say these agents feel what a person feels, or reason as they reason. We say the perceptual and motor limits we impose are real, measured, and reproducible even though the personality is synthetic — mechanical fidelity, not psychological fidelity. It is a smaller promise than the field usually makes, and one we can actually keep.

Why now

Two things changed. Computer-use models that can look at a live page and act on it are now good enough to run a real task end-to-end. And software increasingly ships into a world where agents, not only people, will operate it. That makes a screenshot-in, action-out model the right instrument for this: because it perceives through pixels and acts through coordinates, we control exactly the two channels we want to degrade, and nothing else.

What we're building toward

We want the behavioral check to become a normal part of shipping — something you run before release the way you run a test suite, to see who fails your site before your users do. Not a forecast of conversion, and not a compliance verdict, but a rank-order signal: which page is worse, which fix helped, where the abandonment clusters. If teams could see that as routinely as they see a failing test, the friction that survives to production would be friction someone chose to keep.
