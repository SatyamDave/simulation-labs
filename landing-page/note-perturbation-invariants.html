Perturbation invariants and pipeline cost

Satyam Dave

Technical note · Jul 2026 · Simulation Labs

Abstract. This note collects three offline measurements that let us trust the rest of the system. First, our perturbations are invariant where they must be: image dimensions are preserved in 100% of cases (8/8 personas) and the pipeline is byte-for-byte deterministic across runs (100%), while still destroying 99.95% of image detail for the low-vision persona. Second, the full perception pipeline costs a median of 61.4 ms/frame at 1280×800. Third, our stuck-detector scored perfect precision and recall over 225 synthetic labeled histories. Everything here is offline or on synthetic data, not live sessions.

1 Why invariants matter

The pipeline is only useful if it is repeatable. Two properties have to hold for the coordinate handling and the reporting to mean anything. Perturbations must preserve image dimensions — this is the golden rule that keeps Holo's coordinates from drifting — and, given the same input, they must produce the same output every time, so that a run is evidence rather than an anecdote. This note checks both, then measures what the pipeline costs and how well the outcome classifier separates progress from being stuck. These are properties we can assert offline; they do not depend on the live model.

2 Determinism and dimension preservation

In an offline, deterministic check across all eight personas, every persona's perceive() output was byte-identical across two runs (reproducibility 100%) and matched the input dimensions exactly (dimension preservation 100%, 8/8). Blur and downscale are monotonic in detail loss, and the colour transform is a strict no-op at severity 0.

Preservation does not mean the frame is unchanged — only that its size is. Using the variance of the image Laplacian as a focus measure (base 1412.37 on the fixture), the low-vision persona (blur σ=3.5 combined with downscale ×0.4) retains just 0.05% of that detail — it destroys 99.95% of the high-frequency content of the image — while preserving 100% of the pixel dimensions (640×480).

3 Pipeline cost

We timed perceive() with perf_counter over 80 iterations after 10 warm-up runs; timings are machine-dependent. At 1280×800, a synthetic worst-case persona with all three perception channels enabled at once has a median cost of 61.4 ms/frame (p95 62.8 ms). By channel, the colour transform dominates (39.3 ms), followed by blur (11.3 ms) and downscale (4.6 ms); the real personas, which each enable only a subset, have a median of about 5.1 ms/frame at that size.

To put 61.4 ms in context we compared it against a single Holo inference call, which we treat as roughly 2000 ms. On that basis the full pipeline is under 3.07% of one inference. We flag that the 2000 ms figure is an estimate, to be confirmed by the live latency benchmark; the 61.4 ms measurement stands on its own, but the ratio inherits the estimate's uncertainty.

4 Outcome classifier on synthetic traces

The runner ends a session early when a persona is stuck rather than making progress. We evaluated the stuck-detector offline on 225 synthetic labeled histories (seeded, default_rng(0)): 100 positives — exact-repeat action loops and jittered dead-spot clicks where nothing changes on screen — and 125 negatives — distinct progress, moving clicks (>14px apart), clicks that were close but did produce a reaction, and histories shorter than three steps. It scored precision 1.0, recall 1.0, F1 1.0, with zero false rage-quits (0 false positives). A companion frame-similarity check scored 100% accuracy over 120 synthetic PNG pairs.

These are synthetic labeled traces designed to probe the decision boundary, not recordings of real sessions. They show the classifier behaves as specified on the cases we constructed; they do not establish its accuracy on live traffic.

5 Limitations

Every measurement here is offline or on synthetic data. The invariant and cost checks run on fixtures rather than live pages; the timings are machine-dependent and the inference ratio rests on an unconfirmed 2000 ms estimate. The classifier scores come from labeled synthetic histories we authored, so a perfect score reflects agreement with our own construction, not validated real-world performance. Read this note as evidence that the pipeline is repeatable, cheap, and internally well-behaved — not as a claim about live sessions.
